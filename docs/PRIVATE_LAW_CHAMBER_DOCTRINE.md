# Private Law Chamber Doctrine

## 1. Solely private law

The Chamber only designs private law.

It works through voluntary private legal instruments such as:

- contracts
- membership agreements
- entity bylaws
- association rules
- private charters
- arbitration agreements
- commercial codes
- community covenants
- service agreements

The Chamber does not create public law.

## 2. Host-law subordination

No Chamber rule may override applicable public law.

All Chamber rules, procedures, arbitration frameworks, and membership systems must remain subordinate to the law of the actual jurisdiction where the parties, property, entity, dispute, or conduct exists.

## 3. Consent and exit

Chamber authority exists only through consent.

A person, entity, or community may be governed by Chamber rules only through a lawful private-law instrument, such as a membership agreement, contract, charter, bylaws, service agreement, arbitration clause, or other voluntary adoption mechanism.

Exit rights should be preserved wherever practical and lawful.

## 4. Contract-based authority

The Chamber's work becomes binding only when adopted by consenting parties through private-law instruments.

The Chamber may draft rules, but the binding force comes from agreement.

## 5. Membership-based rules

The Chamber may maintain member rules, committee rules, disciplinary rules, voting or review procedures, and internal codes of conduct.

These are internal private association rules and do not bind non-members.

## 6. Arbitration by agreement

Arbitration must be based on consent.

The Chamber may design arbitration rules, model arbitration clauses, arbitral registry procedures, and review procedures. Actual enforceability depends on applicable law, arbitration statutes, the selected seat, and competent courts.

## 7. Chief Arbitrator

The Chief Arbitrator is appointed by the Chamber.

The Chief Arbitrator administers arbitration under Chamber rules. The Chief Arbitrator does not create public law and does not exercise public authority.

## 8. Special Arbitration Review

Special Arbitration Review is the Chamber's internal appeal or review mechanism for arbitration outcomes within the system.

The Chief Arbitrator sits as institutional head of the review structure. The Chief Arbitrator may appoint or designate the chair of a Review Committee. Other qualified Chamber members may sit as Review Arbiters only when appointed under the Chamber's rules.

## 9. Review arbiters

Committee members do not act as arbiters automatically.

They may act as Review Arbiters only under formal appointment for a specific review matter and only according to Chamber rules.

## 10. No public-law claims

The Chamber must never claim to be:

- a state
- a sovereign government
- a public legislature
- a public court
- a police force
- a taxation authority
- a criminal-law body
- a territorial sovereign

## 11. Summary principle

The Chamber writes private rules for consenting parties. The arbitral system applies those rules. Public law remains supreme.
